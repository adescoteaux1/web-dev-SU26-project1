Hiking

Long-distance trails & mountain routes

Every hike is a systems problem with no undo button. You plan your route, pack your tools, anticipate failure modes — and then the weather changes, the path forks unexpectedly, and you adapt. There's no Stack Overflow on a ridge at 2,000 metres.

resilience planning uncertainty pacing
Transfer to engineering

Hiking taught me to break enormous challenges into the next manageable waypoint — and to keep moving even when the destination isn't yet visible. It's how I approach long-running projects and ambiguous briefs.

02 / 04

Baking

Sourdough, patisserie & the science of rising

Baking is chemistry you can eat. Precise measurement matters enormously — until it doesn't, and intuition takes over. You learn by iteration. A failed loaf is a bug report. You adjust the variables, document what changed, and try again tomorrow.

iteration precision debugging patience
Transfer to engineering

The baking mindset drives my approach to testing and refactoring: change one variable at a time, observe the outcome, document your findings. And understand that some things need time — you can't rush fermentation or a proper code review.

03 / 04

Puzzles

Jigsaws, logic & constraint-based thinking

A 1,000-piece puzzle teaches you that the best strategy isn't always brute force. You work from constraints — the edges, the distinct colours, the unusual shapes. You build islands of certainty and gradually connect them. You learn when to step back and see the bigger picture.

pattern recognition strategy focus constraints
Transfer to engineering

Puzzle-solving sharpened my instinct for constraint-based debugging and architectural decisions. When a system misbehaves, I look for the edges first — the known boundaries — and work inward. It also built my tolerance for sitting with ambiguity.

04 / 04

Reading

Fiction, history, science & everything between

Reading is the only hobby where you inhabit someone else's cognitive architecture. Every author structures information differently, builds arguments at different speeds, and assumes a different reader. The more you read across genres, the more flexible your own thinking becomes.

empathy communication mental models perspective
Transfer to engineering

Heavy reading made me a better writer of documentation, a better listener in requirements meetings, and a better communicator of technical complexity to non-technical stakeholders. Understanding narrative is understanding how humans process information.

Specific transfers
The crossover map

These aren't vague analogies — they're specific cognitive and behavioural skills that have measurably changed how I work. The classroom and the workplace are rarely where we learn the deepest lessons about how to think.

Hiking → Scope management Learning to abandon a summit when conditions change — without ego — maps directly to killing a feature that isn't working.
Baking → Reproducible builds The baker's obsession with consistent conditions translates into rigorous environment management and CI/CD discipline.
Puzzles → API design Thinking in edges and constraints first produces cleaner interfaces. Define the boundary conditions before the happy path.
Reading → Code review culture A reader who respects the writer gives better feedback. Empathy with the original author makes code reviews constructive, not combative.
Hiking → Incident response When something goes wrong at altitude, panic is expensive. The same composure that gets you safely off a mountain steadies you through a production outage.
Baking + Reading → Technical writing Precision from the kitchen, narrative from the page. Good documentation is both chemically accurate and readable by a human.

On education,
identity,
and success

There's a persistent myth that the path to technical excellence is a straight line: study computer science, practise algorithms, ship code. As if the mind were a muscle that responds only to one kind of exercise.

The reality is that our most complex work is built from the full breadth of who we are. The engineer who hikes brings physical discipline and environmental awareness. The one who bakes brings experimental rigour and an understanding that time is a variable. The puzzle-solver brings strategic patience. The reader brings perspective.

Education and career success aren't diminished by having a rich inner life outside work — they're enabled by it. The skills we develop in play are often more durable than the ones we develop under pressure, because we learned them for their own sake.

I don't list these hobbies to seem well-rounded on a profile. I list them because they are genuinely, materially part of how I think — and because I believe that the best version of any professional is the one who shows up as a whole person.
